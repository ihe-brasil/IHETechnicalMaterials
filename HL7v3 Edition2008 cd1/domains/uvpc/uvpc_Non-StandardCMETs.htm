Introduction

This domain uses "internal" CMETs (as opposed to the universal COCT variety) to provide reuse of model elements within the domain. In the current DSTU release, there are two of these internal CMETs that are not yet normative (or DSTU), but which are included as references in designs that are part of the DSTU. This topic serves as the place-holder for these two CMETs.

Care Plan Structure Overview:

Among the most important Care Structures in the Care Provision Domain is the Care Plan Structure. The purpose of this structure is to define the management action plans for the various conditions identified for the target of care. It is the structure in which the care planning for all individual professions or for groups of professionals can be organized, planned and checked for completion. Communicating explicitly documented and planned actions and goals greatly aids the team in understanding and coordinating the actions that need to be performed for the person. Care plans also permit the monitoring and flagging of unperformed activities and unmet goals for later follow up.

The term Patient Care Planning in MESH is described as:

Usually a written medical and nursing care program designed for a particular patient. Year introduced: 1968"

The basic theory underlying this structure comes from Larry Weeds Problem-oriented Medical Record, also introduced in the 1960s, which is used by many health professions and electronic health record systems as a primary method for organizing work and documentation.

Phases of Care Plans and Guidelines: Essential to many Care Plans and Guidelines is the concept of sequential "Phases" or stages. These phases of the Care Plan are to be modeled using recursive component ActRelationship on the focal act of Care Provision. Each component phase is then considered a mini-guideline or mini-care plan. Typical phases include those defined by nursing practice, e.g. assessment, plan, performance and evaluation; or those defined by organizational practice, e.g. pre-operative, operative, post-operative recovery, post-operative rehabilitation, etc. (see Figure 1) The decision support tools discussed below govern entrance and exit logic from these mini-guidelines and care plans.

Scope of Responsibility:

The Care Provision Structure is attached to the Care Provision Act in two ways. As components of the Care Provision Act, the Care Plans reflect part of the scope of responsibility (a component of the care delivery) defined by the Care Provision Act (see the Care Provision Domain narrative). Component is more specific than pertinent. It says that one act is part of another act. In this case, planning for future care is "part of" the delivery of care, which is the focal class. As pertinent information to the Care Provision Act, the Care Plans are used by the receiver to make sure that orders are checked for interactions and not duplicated or mistakenly discontinued by the receiver.

For example, if a request is made to a receiver to answer a particular question on the care of a particular disease, that question may be expressed within a Care Plan request. A particular question may be asked of the receiver of the request to fill in the observation.value field and/or the observation.text field or other relevant fields in an instance of observation. To further illustrate this example; the patient may have a cardiac valve replacement and be anti-coagulated on coumadin. The patient may also be pregnant, which is noted as pertinent information. A cardiologist may be asked for the highest acceptable Protime INR value that would limit bleeding in the pregnancy but still give protection for the valve replacement. In this case, the component care plan would identify the cardiac valve replacement as the reason for the care plan. The Care Plan Class instance would be in request mood. The observation.mood would be goal request; the observation.code would code the Protime test; and, as in all requests, the values for the acceptable INR range are not included, indicating that they should be filled in by the cardiologist (see Table 1).

Moods and States of Care Plans and Guidelines: The difference between the Guideline Class and the Care Plan Class is the difference in concept between a Guideline, which is "a general list of actions that are defined to be applied to all appropriate targets of care when certain pre-conditions are met" versus the Care Plan, which is "the specific list of actions that are intended to be applied to a specific target of care." Therefore, the mood code of Guidelines is Definition Mood, and the mood code of Care Plans is Intent Mood and its children moods (See table 2).

Table 2: Children of Intent Mood (link to ActMood file in RIM)



A Request would be used to ask a consultant to recommend a care plan. Proposal would be used by a consultant recommending a Care Plan. Intent itself would be used to activate a care plan. Care plans may be scheduled in Appointment mood. For each mood, several states are valid, in particular the states of "active" and "completed. The one or more care plan instances in a given communication potentially contain any acts included in the Care Statement Structure.

Typically, a specific Care Plan will have actions added or taken away from the list of actions in a Guideline, often referred to as Care Plan variations. In relationship to clinical pathways, such variations are normally documented in order to measure the variances on populations of patients, which can be used to improve the guidelines. For the purpose of clarity within the Care Plan Structure, variation is defined here as something that is different from a standard (i.e., the Guideline), where variance is a statistical measure of dispersion, or a calculated degree of variation (How much does this patient differ compared to a group of similar patients). Of course the reasons for these Care Plan variations include the presence of co-morbidities, judgments of the clinician, non-consent of a patient, and other reasons described in the disease management literature.

Often, in a Care Provision communication, the Guideline is named simply as a reference to an external guideline without specifically representing all the Care Statements implied by the Guideline. However, other business needs may require that all the Care Statements that exist in a specific Guideline definition be specifically communicated as well. It should go without saying that in a Care Provision communication, the Care Statements collected by a Guideline need to be kept completely separate for patient safety reasons from Care Statements collected by a Care Plan. However, it is important to be able to document the variation between what the Guideline (in Definition Mood) states should be done, and what according to the Care Statements has actually been done (Any Act in Event Mood). In the Care Plan RMIM, what "has been done" is documented in the "fulfillment" ActRelationship from Care Statements CMET to the Care Plan choice box.

Moods and States of Care Plan Components: As mentioned above, Care Plans are collectors of Care Statements. Like the Care Plan itself, these Care Statements collected by the Care Plan will have moods of either Intent or the children of Intent. However, like any Care Statements in the Intent moods, instances of these Care Statements will be created as the Act concept moves through the downstream stages of the business cycle of Acts, e.g. Events. As mentioned above, the Fulfillment ActRelationships to the Care Plan component Acts represents the downstream instances in the Act business cycle.

Between the use of Definition mood in the Guideline Class Components, the Intent moods in the Care Plan Class Components, the Acts in Predicate moods related to the Care Plans and Components, and the Fulfillment Acts, all the options for moodCode can be used in the Care Plan Structure, thus giving an overview on the dynamics of a whole set of (specialized) Acts planned or performed during Care Provision. Clinician thus can track the status of their work carried out on behalf of Patients or other Targets of Care.

Goals in Care Plans: Goals are common within Care Plan phases, and often must be met in order to move a patient from one phase of a Care Plan to another. For example, for a patient to move from the post-operative recovery room to a surgical floor bed, certain vital signs must be stable. Whether these goals are considered met or not met may depend on clinician judgment or on decision support rules stored in Observation.derivationExpr. A typical goal is expressed as an Observation scheduled for some time in the future with a particular value in the observation.value field. For example, a rule may state that the observation of a heart rate between 60 and 100 must be present at the time of discharge from recovery. A Care Plan may identify this heart rate as a goal for 30 minutes after entry to the recovery unit.

Order Sets in Care Plans: Order sets can organize and structure complex health care plans into useful units of work for clinicians. An order within an order set can request any clinical activity including referrals (requests for encounters), acts, supplies, procedures and observations among others. Structured observations and goal setting can also be supported from the order set. The sharing of care protocols and regimes authored as phased order sets has a much broader audience for work on error reduction, standardization of care and quality improvement. Order sets in this context can be envisioned as action elements within a care plan, allowing a clinician to organize and request a step in a larger protocol.

Definition of the structure and function of orders and order sets for an implementation-independent EHR referred to as the virtual medical record (vMR) is a central issue in development of interoperable functionality necessary to publicaly published guidelines that may be used by multiple institutions. HL7 Clinical Decision Support Technical Committee is in the process of describing a multi-layered standard that supports the publication and maintenance of order set libraries, the sharing of order sets between collaborating institutions, the structuring of order sets to support effective clinical use, and the interoperation of order sets within advanced clinical guideline support software. Organizations employing this standard would deploy the depth of specification appropriate to their enterprise needs and the capabilities of their clinical systems architecture and performance.

Within a specific phase or order set, the detail of how care is intended to be delivered is expressed. This detail includes the expression of goals and measurements of outcomes after interventions are applied. Order sets specifically include particular sequences of orders and decision support rules, including rules for stopping or revising orders and rules for entering or exiting the current order set in order to enroll the patient in a new phase of the care plan.

Clinical Decision Support Rules in Care Plans: Management of the flow between phases of a care plan is implemented via decision support rules expressed in instances of the observation class.. These rules, in whatever decision support language is used, may be expressed in the Observation.derivationExpr attribute in the observation class included as a component of the current phase of a Care Plan. Typically the rule evaluates current observations on the patient against goals expressed for the current phase of care and determines whether the patient is ready to move on to the next phase of care.

Further guidance on the use of these decision support languages will be published by the HL7 Clinical Decision Support Technical Committee. Examples used in Care Provision models include expressions as simple as formulae for calculations, such as summing up a total score, or as complicated as making a choice between two potential actions based on decision criteria expressed in the derivation expression logic syntax.

Care Plan Structure Walk-through:

CarePlan Focal Class:The Care Plan Structure's focal class act represents the care plan.

classCode: The vocabulary domain specified for Act.classCode includes PCPR (Patient Care Provision) as a specialization of the Act class.

moodCode: The Care Plan Act may be expressed in many moods. These moods distinguish the different aspects of the business lifecycle of a Care Plan Act. These moods for the Care Plan are defined as follows:
DEF (definition) Definition mood code can be used to express a guideline or protocol. This can include treatment protocols, standardized care plans, clinical pathways or similar.

INT (intent)

Intent can be used to express the intended activities in a care plan. In particular the appropriate selection of observations and activities from a guideline that are included in the care plan for one particular patient. So the intended Acts are individualized from the guideline.

GOL (Goal)

Expectation to make a specific observation with a desired value at a predefined future time.

In the clinical pathway, the goal can be put against the actual value (the observation in event mood). If there is a difference, it is the individual variation of the patient against the goal. If a population of patients is measured against the goal, and the average is calculated, it is the variance.

RQO (request)

A "Care Plan Request" is a question to another care provider regarding their ability to accept the responsibility of a Care Plan for the subject. A Request would be used for instance to ask a consultant to recommend a care plan. The scope of responsibility including CareProvision.code, reasons for care provision, and component care plans that set the specific expectations for care apply to the scope of responsibility for the receiver of the request.

PRP (Proposal)

Proposal would be used by a consultant recommending a Care Plan. Intent itself would be used to activate a care plan. Care plans may be scheduled in Appointment mood.

ActMoodPredicate

Any of the above service moods (e.g., event, intent, or goal) can be turned into a predicate used as a criterion to express conditionals (or queries.) However, currently we allow only criteria on service events.

id: This attribute identifies a particular Care Plan.

derivationExpr: The derivation expression attribute allows a string expression on derivation and is supported in an observation instance. It is used in Care Plan to give instructions (Definition mood), or to explain how a value has been determined (event mood). Examples often used include the summing up of individual scores to a sumscore, e.g. for the Apgar score or Barthel index.

title: One title can be given to the Care Plan in free generated text. This can be done in the definition mood to refer to a guideline or pathway, and in the intent mood to refer to a particular care plan for a target.

statusCode: The statusCode attribute is used to indicate the current state of the Care Plan. The statusCode attribute corresponds to the Act state machine and for the Care Plan has the following valid values:

Code

Definition

active

The care plan can be performed or is being performed.

suspended

An care plan that has been activated (actions could or have been performed against it), but has been temporarily disabled. No further action should be taken against it until it is resumed.

completed

A care plan that has terminated normally after all of its constituents have been performed.

aborted

The care plan has been terminated prior to the originally indended completion.

obsolete

This care plan has been replaced by a new instance, e.g., due to changes, goals not met etc.

nullify

This care plan was created in error and has been 'removed' and is treated as though it never existed. A record is retained for audit purposes only.

The other attributes have the common Act attributes characteristics that are explained in detail at the D-MIM level.

Participations from Care Plan

The Care Plan focal class has several participations to R_AssignedPerson, R_AssignedParty and R_AssignedEntity. These have similar meaning as in the D-MIM and are explained there.

Important here however is that the R_AssignedEntity in the author participation can be the patient her or himself. This for those instances that patients regulate their own treatment and / or care.

Further, the Performer participation uses the functionCode to distinguish between the Care Coordinator and other care team members. We do need new ParticipationFunction code for Care Coordinator. Component Relationship

The component relationship from Care Plan Act to itself functions to phasing the care plan trough the different phases.

Guideline Act Relationship

The focal class Care Plan has a Definition relationship to the Guideline Class. The type code of the Act Relationship is INST (instantiates). It is used in the context of the Care Plan to capture the link between the Guideline (the master part) and the actual service, where this is the actual care plan for a particular patient. The instantiation in the Care Plan may override the Guidelines contents via removing, altering or adding to the care plan.

Guideline

The Guideline's attributes are similar to the Care Plan except for the mood code which is always in DEF (Definition) and the text attribute that allows to give full written explanations about the guideline, e.g. the scientific evidence for it's use, reference to the literature, and instructions for a professional how to deal with it.

The guideline has an author participation with R_AssignedEntity who is responsible for the content.

The guideline has also a component relationship to link different instances of the guideline together or to link different components from the guideline choice box.

Guideline Component Relationship

This kind of relationship has been explained at D-MIM level and is used here in the standard way. A guideline can consist of several components from the Defined Care choice box.

Defined Care choice box.

This choice box holds several of the classes of the Care Statement structure. All instances in the Defined Care Choice Box have the mood code Definition (DEF).

Patient Instructions

This act Patient Instructions holds the relevant instructions for the patient that are derived from or associated with the guideline and the guidelines. The attributes are similar to generic act.

{The class code PROC is subject to change given ongoing discussions on this matter}

Final Goal

Via the final ActRelationship the Act Class ObservationGoal is associated to the Care Plan. The function of the ObservationGoal is to set goals in the Care Plan. It works like this: If an Observation at a point in time (that is expressed in a care statement observation) has a particular value, the clinician can state a better value as goal and Plan activities in the Care Plan to achieve the goal and thus to improve the situation for the patient. Alternatively, the Guideline on which the Care Plan can be based can state a reference value for a particular observation and the ObservationGoal can be used to define that in the Care Plan for the individual patient (Target).

In the event mood of this observation (that is then the 2nd instantiation for this observation, where goal is the 1st), the actual value is present and can be compared to the ObservationGoal value. The variation can also be determined, which is a third instantiation of the observation, related to the goal and to the actual value.

PlannedCare Choice Box

The PlannedCare choice box is connected to the Care Plan via a component relationship, indicating that a care plan can have 0 to many components in it.

In the Planned Choice box, all the different Act classes are in Intent mood (INT) to indicate what activities, observations, encounters, and substance administrations, etc. are planned in the Care Plan for an individual.

The PlannedCare choice box has an inFulfillment Act relationship with the Guideline choicebox. This can be used to determine that an act in the PlannedCare choice box fulfills the suggested act in the guideline.

{Future work for the Care Plan will include further expression of the exact mechanics for this relationship, including the explanations for it, and the changes of moods and acts status}.

Criterion

The PlannedCare choice box has a act relationship named 'conditions' to the Act Criterion. The function of Criterion is explained in depth elsewhere in the D-MIM. It functions here in a similar way: to set limits and criteria to particular acts.

Material

Via the consumable participation the SubstanceAdministrationIntent Act class in the PlannedCare choice box has a direct link to the Role Choicebox Material, in which the roles AdministrableMaterial and Medication can be selected and thus the Entity MaterialKind and via the MaterialPart role to MaterialKind2. MaterialKind is constrained to class codes PART or INGR (ingredient) It functions similarly as in the Care Statement and allows to determine in the Care Plan what substance should be given at what time.

ConditionTracking

Via the 'reason' relationship the PlannedCare choice box is linked to the ConditionTracking CMET.

Care Statement

The Care Statement (as CMET expressed here to link to the whole structure in the R-MIM) is linked to the PlannedCare choice box via the fulfillment relationship. This functions such that the planned care acts in intent mood and the care statement acts that are in event mood are linked to each other.

The moodcode of the Care Statement here is constrained to INT.

ObservationGoal

Similar to the Care Plan Act, the Goal Observation is linked to the PlannedCare choice box (as a shadow). Its function is the same here as described above for Care Plan.

Planned Review

In clinical care, a care plan is reviewed regularly. This can be done on ad hoc basis, e.g. based on changes in the condition of the patient, or when particular observations are present as care statement. However, it is also possible to have a predetermined review of the care plan. For example in elderly care, the care plan will not change very often, but still would require an annual or 6 month review.

The class code is REV for review, the mood is Intent and the effective time is the date of the reassessment.

Performer

The AssignedPerson that carries out the acts from the PlannedCare choice box is linked to it via the Performer participation.

Annotation

It is possible to add annotations to the Care Plan via the subject of relationship. Annotation in relation to PlannedCare has an Act class code, the EVN moodcode, the text attribute to document the annotation and the statuscode is completed to indicate that the annotation is ready.

Annotation has also an author (shadow from the author of the Care Plan).
