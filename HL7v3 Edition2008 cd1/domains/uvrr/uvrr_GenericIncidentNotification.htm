Introduction

Generic Incident Notification

The Generic Incident Notification is a generalised, high level, yet encompassing report for the capture of information, in particular the circumstances surrounding an unplanned event or potential event that constitutes a Patient Safety Incident. These incidents can be reported by individuals involved in the process of care. The main purpose of using the GIN message is to gather snapshot information of the profile of a Patient Safety Incident (what, when, where, who) for the purpose of aggregation, analysis and learning, not specifically for the follow-up of individual reports.

A patient safety incident is defined as any unintended, expected or unexpected incident(s) that could have or did lead to harm for one or more patients receiving health care services. [Note: staff incidents are currently not reportable via this model.] Reports can cover actual patient safety incidents, including those caused by acts of omission, and prevented patient safety incidents (near misses). The reported data is then aggregated and analysed by health care agencies to identify areas of concern that warrant further investigation. The Generic Incident Notification not intended to support a detailed investigation of an incident (this will be covered by a future R-MIM - Patient Safety: Root causes and Underlying factors) nor necessarily to comply with regulatory reporting requirements.

Examples of incidents that would be reportable via the Patient Safety Generic Incident Notification are delays in access to care, medication errors, device failures, slip/trip/fall, patient misidentification, verbal/racial/sexual abuse directed towards a patient.

The Generic Incident Notification message model is described in the RMIM Walkthrough below; it is based around the concept of the report itself (the Incident Investigation), the Incident as a whole, and the Incident as it affects individual patient(s), and the Clinical Act(s) that were involved in the incident. All key acts apart from the Clinical Act(s) are in the mood of event, because they are describing a report or an incident that has occurred.

The "reporting" nature of this messaging is such that the dynamic model for this topic is simple. There is a sending system which informs and a receiving system that tracks that information. Trigger events are user-based, being activated when the user takes the decisison that a report, or a revised report, is complete; and the only state used is that of "complete". The main message interaction is therefore used to send an initial Patient Safety Generic Incident Notification, and a supplmentary message interaction is available to describe a revision of previously sent information which may be implemented if and when local business rules allow revision/update. Apart from system level acknowledgement, no other information flows occur.

Differentiation from other Incident and Public Health Messaging Topics

The Generic Incident Notification is distinct from the Individual Case Safety Report as it captures high-level information on a wide variety of patient focused incidents, which can include incidents affecting a group of patients. In contrast, the Individual Case Safety Report is designed to support regulatory reporting of adverse incidents associated with product safety. These products relate primarily to blood, medicines, including vaccines and devices.

The Generic Incident Notification also differs from Case or Outbreak Management. Case or Outbreak Management topics are intended to cover public health cases of reportable conditions e.g. infectious diseases.

Please note: The documentation for this topic has been written in UK English.

 4.1.1.1Patient Monitoring Failure (PORR_SN150001UV01)

Baby Ben Blue was born prematurely in Good Health Hospital at a gestation of 28 weeks; he is now four weeks old, is in the Special Care Baby Unit and is doing well, but still requires ventilation due to poorly developed lungs.

On this particular day, Ben required a chest x-ray; this involved moving him to position the x-ray plate underneath him. To prevent the cardiac monitor and ventilator from alarming all the time while he is being re-positioned, the nurse "silenced" the alarms.

Once the x-ray was completed, the nurse and radiographer left the nursery to attend to another baby, but Baby Ben's cardiac monitor and ventilator alarms were not be re-set to sound.

During Baby Ben's repositioning at the end of the x-ray, a piece of the ventilator tubing had become disconnected; this resulted in Benbeing deprived of ventilatory support.

On returning to Baby Ben's room, the nurse noted that the cardiac monitor showed a heart rate of 40 and that Ben was cyanosed. She immediately identified the problem and reconnected the ventilator tubing. Baby Ben made continued to grow and develop, although it was found that he that he had suffered a small intracranial haemorrhage, which was thought to be due to the hypoxia suffered whilst the ventilator tubing had become disconnected.

The Charge Nurse for the SCBU fills in a Patient Safety Incident report form with details of Baby Ben Blue's incident, but without mentioning the patient's name, on the Risk Management System at Good Health Hospital. This system then automatically sends a report to the national Patient Safety Agency which collates and analyses all such reports and feeds back information to improve patient safety.

 4.1.2.1Incorrect Procedure Performed (PORR_SN150002UV01)

A 70 year old man was admitted to hospital for a right nephrectomy. The patient signed a consent form giving the Urologist permission to perform such an operation. The next day, the patient's X-rays were reviewed and an entry was made in the Department diary for a left nephrectomy. This information was then re-entered on the Operating List.

The patient was admitted to theatre and a left nephrectomy was performed. After the patient came round, he was confused and lethargic, which prompted the Nurse to perform checks with the Urologist to ensure that his remaining kidney was functioning normally. It was during these checks that they discovered that the wrong kidney had been removed. The patient was immediately put on a dialysis machine to stabilise his condition. The next day the Nurse notified the Risk Manager that an incident had occurred.

The Risk Manager then reported the incident to the Patient Safety Centre stating poor communication and the mislabelling of X-Rays as proximate factors.

The Risk Manager at the aforementioned health care organisation decided upon subsequent consideration of the incident to change one of the proximate factors in the report to 'team factors'. He logged onto his local system within the time constraints set by local practice and changed a cached copy of the report. This revised incident notification was then submitted to the Patient Safety Centre where it was reconciled with the original record before the locally applied deadline.

Description 
Structured Name:  Generic Incident Notification Event Completion Informer

An application that is capable of providing notification data on a patient safety incident to another application. This could typically be a local risk management system used in a care environment, that is responsible for informing appropriate central organisations or agencies of the incident.

4.2.2Generic Incident Notification Receiver  (PORR_AR150002UV01)
Description 
Structured Name:  Generic Incident Notification Event Completion Tracker

An application that is capable of receiving patient safety incident notification reports from another application. This would typically be a system used by an appropriate central organisation or agency for the collection of patient safety incident data. Since this application role is of the stereotype "tracker", no formal receiver responsibilities for this are currently defined other than to send a system level acknowledgement on receipt of the message.

RMIM Introduction

The Patient Safety Generic Incident Notification (GIN) RMIM is a high-level notification message, which captures information about any unintended, expected or unexpected incident(s) that could have or did lead to harm for one or more patients receiving health care services. As well as the reporting of incidents which affect an individual and cause harm, this definition includes:

1. Incidents that affect several patients

2. Incidents that have the potential to cause harm, but which do not adversely affect a patient

3. Incidents that are the result of "acts of omission"

As the GIN is a high-level notification message, only information that is readily accessible soon after an incident is captured in it. The information model assumes that there will be an electronic health record or similar to populate information about a patient's clinical condition using the clinical statement structure (A_CareStatementUniversal CMET). The model also assumes that message implementers would want to have a choice as to the level of identifiable information they wish to capture.

RMIM Walkthrough

The GIN RMIM has four acts that form its main structural elements; these are the Investigation Event, the Incident Event, the Individual Patient Incident and the Supporting Clinical Statement that is a component of the Incident Event.

The Investigation Event is the entry class for the message, and describes that this is a report of a Patient Safety Incident, and it is from this class that the information about the Author of the report (and indeed the performer of any investigation that results in the report) is described. The Investigation Event links to the Incident Event class by means of a "Reason" relationship; without the Patient Safety Incident having occurred (or being a "near miss"), there would be no reason to have a report to notify an agency about it.

The Incident Event class describes some core information about the Patient Safety incident as a whole. This includes its type, when it occurred and any textual description of the incident. Associated with the Incident Event class are various classes to describe where the incident occurred, who were participants in it, and what form that participation took, how the incident was discovered, whether there were any factors that might be considered to have caused the incident (and if so, what if anything has been done to mitigate those things), any actions that were taken as a consequence of the incident as a whole, and whether the incident occurred in relation to a research study, such as a clinical trial. The Incident Event also has an association to describe if a group of patients, who are not identified separately, were involved in a patient safety incident.

The Assigned Entity CMET is used with the Participation into the Incident Event to describe any required information about the entity (person) who has been "assigned" to participate in (be employed in) the healthcare environment in which the whole Patient Safety Incident occurred, and thus who had a participation in the Patient Safety Incident in some way (including indirectly, e.g. as a witness). This is because the focus of the GIN is to collect information from healthcare staff about Patient Safety Incidents, so the role to be described is the staff's "assigned function" within the healthcare environment.

The Choice Box giving options about who performed the Discovery of the Patient Safety Incident is designed to capture only the type (role) of the person who made the discovery (a caregiver, a healthcare professional/member of staff, or a patient (who may be involved in the incident themselves, or an observer of it). This structure is not designed to describe any further detail about the "discoverer" since there is (currently) no requirement for this.

The Individual Patient Incident class that is linked to the Incident Event class by a component relationship, allows description of an incident as it relates to an individual patient, who may or may not be identified. Associated with the incident as it related to one or more individual patients, there are classes that describe more specifically who was responsible for this patient at the time of the incident, any actions that were taken to minimise the effect of the incident on this patient, any actions that were taken to help the patient recover from the incident, and to describe the effect(s) of the incident on this patient. In addition, for a "near miss" situation, there is the facility to describe any actions that were taken to "block" the patient from suffering harm from the incident. There are also structures to describe if the patient was the subject of a care pathway at the time of the incident, how the patient can be described in terms of particular age bandings used in safety incident analysis, and, by means of the Clinical Statement structure, to describe any relevant information about the patient's clinical condition at the time of the incident.

The fourth key structure in the GIN message is the Clinical Statement CMET that is associated with the Incident Event by means of a "component of" relationship. One or more iterations of the structure should be used to describe the clinical circumstances/detail of the patient safety incident, either in text or in structured form.

Optionality and Conformance The PSGIN RMIM as currently modelled for international DSTU status; as such, all the cardinalities are set to be as relaxed as possible and with conformance set as optional for all classes, relationships and attributes except those for which the structures would make no sense without the information.
