Purpose

The purpose of this storyboard is to illustrate the request and report messaging as well as documents related to Cystic Fibrosis testing.

 2.1.1.1Cystic Fibrosis (POCG_SN000020UV01)

Summary:

  1. Patient meets criteria for Cystic Fibrosis (CF) testing.

  2. Informed consent signed.

  3. Tests ordered.

  4. Results received.

  5. Results explained.

  6. Plan drafted.

Detail:

The patient's ethnicity, medical history, and/or family history (as documented in chart ) trigger the consideration of genetic testing for CF mutations. The CF test is explained to the patient (documented), the patient's questions are answered (documented), and the informed consent is signed. The doctor uses an electronic order entry system (same as for other lab tests) to order CF testing (documented). The lab system receives the order and forwards it to the Molecular Genetics System (MGS). A blood sample is drawn and sent to the Molecular Genetics Laboratory. The test is performed and results are provided by a lab technologist on a "worksheet". The Molecular Laboratory's Genetic Counselor reviews the results and the pertinent history and then creates a report in MGS including the reason for referral, testing method, results, and interpretation (documented). The results and report are reviewed by the Molecular Laboratory Consultant and released. The report is sent from MGS to the lab system and also to the EMR system for viewing by the doctor. The physician and patient discuss the test results (documented), and appropriate plans are outlined ( documented).

Presentation:

Pat Patient is being seen at Care Clinic by Dr. Doorman. Dr. Doorman reviews Pat's EHR, ethnicity, medical history, and family history and determines that Pat should be screened for Cystic Fibrosis mutations. Dr. Doorman answers Pat's questions and Pat signs an informed consent form.

Dr. Doorman orders a Cystic Fibrosis test through Orders Plus (Electronic Order Entry System). Lab Plus receives the order and forwards it to Molecular Genetics Plus (Molecular Genetics System).

Anita Nurse takes a blood sample from Pat and sends it to Molecular Lab Plus (Molecular Genetics Lab).

Molly Molecular (Lab Technologist) receives the blood specimen and the test order and performs the Cystic Fibrosis test. She records the results on a worksheet.

Carla Counselor (Molecular Genetics Counselor) reviews the results and Pat's history and creates a report in Molecular Genetics Plus.

Cory Consultant reviews Carla's work, makes any necessary modifications, and releases the report to Molecular Genetics Plus and Lab Plus.

Dr. Doorman looks at Pat's results in the EHR. Dr. Doorman and Pat meet to discuss the results and appropriate next steps.

Triggers:

  • Family history

  • Patient has a suspected diagnosis

  • Carrier screening (routinely offered to all pregnant couples)

  • Male infertility (CBAVD - Congenital Bilateral Absence of the Vas Deferens)

  • Ethnicity

Data:

  1. Ethnicity, Medical history, and Family history risk factors. Examples:

    • Positive sweat chloride test

    • Pulmonary and/or pancreatic symptoms

    • European ancestry

    • Affected family member

  2. Informed consent document.

    • Supplementary educational materials

    • Electronic patient signature

  3. Lab order

    • Orderable in formulary

    • Test prerequisites

    • Protocol/workflow

  4. Lab result

    • 33 mutations (greater than 900 documented mutations in the CF gene)

    • mutation name

    • location (specific exon or intron)

    • Interpretation with explanation, considering

    • reason for referral

    • ethnicity

    • family history

2.1.2Pharmacogenomics-Based Clinical Trial & Submission  (POCG_ST000030UV01)

Purpose

The purpose of this storyboard is to illustrate the request and report messaging as well as documents related to Pharmacogenomics-based Clinical Trial & submission.

Diagram

Link to wide graphic (opens in a new window)

 2.1.2.1Pharmacogenomics (POCG_SN000030UV01)

Scenario Summary

  1. Clinical trial designs with pharmacogenomics components.

  2. IRB approval.

  3. Patient case report form (CRF) and informed consent form design.

  4. Patient recruitment and DNA or tissue sample receiving.

  5. Gene selection and variation markers discovery.

  6. Regulation compliant clinical genotyping.

  7. Clinical demographic, clinical phenotype, and lab test results received.

  8. Haplotype or other types of marker assignment to the patients.

  9. Statistical analysis to establish the association of the genetic markers to the clinical outcomes.

  10. Validation study.

  11. Reports generation and communicated between pharmaceutical and biotechnology partners, or submission materials compiled for regulatory agencies.

Workflow And Process

Clinical trials with a therapeutic product or device targeting to the global or niche pharmaceutical industry area requires the understanding of the genetic structure of the potential patient population and its interaction with the drug or treatment. Therefore the design of the trial involves the pharmacogenomics component. For example, the trial has to consider the patient population in terms of geo-ethno background recruited into the trial and the other covariates that stems from the genomics science. The sample size needs to be determined based on the understanding or estimate of the gene variation markers in terms of the allele frequency (common vs. rare), genetic model (recessive vs. dominant), and strength of the marker prediction power.

As with any traditional types of trials, the pharmacogenomics based trials also need to obtain the regulatory approval and the patient enrollment needs to follow the standard and GCP (good clinical practice) procedures, such as using IRB approval of the design, obtaining patient consent forms for the genomics work, etc. The approved clinical research centers could be used to collect the patient clinical data during the dosage dependent trials and the other monitoring and diagnostics lab testing will be performed at the accredited labs.

Patient DNA samples extracted from the relevant tissues will be sent to the clinical regulatory compliant genotyping facilities. Genotyping work will be applied to these DNA sample using the pharmacogenomics markers selected for the trials. The genotype results will be sent to the data management databases of the trial and statistical analysis will be performed to discover the associations between the genetics markers and the clinical outcomes and endpoints.

The discovery results will be then compiled into the clinical trial reports that can be used for further drug development. It can also be integrated into the submission packages used for the product application and approval to the regulatory bodies.

Trigger Events

  • DNA Banking from the previous trials advocates the use of genomics technology to profile the compound and population.

  • Previous trials or market information indicates the divided responses in the target population to the class of drugs or treatments.

  • Characterization of the drug target(s) reveals prevalence of the genetic variation in the gene or pathways involved in the mode of action (MOA) of the drug or disease susceptibility or progression.

  • Ab initio trial with pharmacogenomics design.

Data

  1. Patient demographic and clinical observations as reported in CRF (case report form):

    • Age.

    • Gender.

    • Ethnicity.

    • Geographic origins.

    • Dosage.

    • Confounding disease or drug usage.

    • Clinic visit observations: BMI, blood pressure, etc. depending on the therapeutic areas.

  2. Informed consent document.

    • Genetic testing education materials.

    • Electronic patient signature.

  3. Lab test

    • Test prerequisites.

    • Protocol/workflow

    • Test result.

  4. Genomics data

    • Selected genes

      • Functional annotation of the genes.

      • Genomic structure

      • Transcript structure (optional)

      • Protein structure (optional)

      • SNPs

      • Haplotypes

    • Genotyping data

      • Minimal SNP selection

      • SNP marker assay design

      • Assay validation and production

      • HapLocus building and haplotype assignments

      • Allele frequencies.

  5. Association Analysis:

    • Hypothesis generation: marker model (dominant vs. recessive), gene model (mono vs. multiple)

    • Statistics generation: marker raw p values, adjusted p values, odds ratios; regression, multiple comparison, and permutations.

    • Marker quality control

    • Biological story building

  6. Reports

  7. Validation

  8. Submission

Model Walk-Through

The model is focused on genomic data in the clinical trial arena. It is important to note at this point that all parts of this model that are not related to genomic data could be replaced by models developed by the HL7 RCRIM TC (Regulated Clinical Research Information Management) that focuses on clinical trials at large. The 'non-genomic' parts of this model were developed for the sake of better understanding of the genomic-based processes (e.g., gene selection and haplotype discovery), and for completeness of the model that might make it more convenient to read. Both HL7 groups hold joint meetings and try to harmonize their models. A dedicated mechanism in HL7 for sharing parts of models across different groups (domains) is the CMET mechanism (Common Message Element Type). A CMET model is an HL7 model that can be incorporated into another model in a modular way. The key for using CMETs effectively is clean modularity. In the HL7 CG group we have been trying to develop a Genotype CMET - an atomic unit that could be plugged into any model that needs to convey genomic data. So, the Pharmacogenomics DIM model introduced here is in fact a prototyping framework in which we use the HL7 CG Genotype CMET on the one hand and on the other hand we strive to harmonize it with the HL7 RCRIM models (CDISC-based).

Having said that, we could start the walk-through of the Pharmacogenomics model: The left side of the model has the main participants in this model, i.e., the sponsor (Pharma), the CRO and the Patient. There are 3 more participants I the model (Device, Collaborator and regulator) and they will be described further on.

  • At the upper left corner we can see the patient object and its related information. Note that this model follows the HL7 V3 RIM (Reference Information Model) core classes and associations. For example, we have a Person entity (green box) who plays the role (yellow box) of a Patient with participation (cyan box) of being the subject of the PahrmacogenomicsClinicalTrial Act.
  • At the middle left we can see the CRO recruiting patients - a process represented by the receiving the consent form for which the patient has the participation of legalAuthenticator.
  • At the lower left we can see the Pharma entity taking the role of ClinicalResearchSponsor which has the participation of Performer of the PharmacogenomicsTrialDesign act.

On the right side of the model and continuing to the bottom we can find the analysis and reporting process starting from the Pharmacogenomics trial results and its analysis, on to the CRO, and further to the sponsor. From there we can see the beginning of the submission process to the regulator with the validation and other necessary actions.

At the heart of the model we find the genomic acts, starting from the top where we can see the main act - the PharmacogenomicsClinicalTrial, associated with the PharmacogenomicsTesting act (which is an attempt to generalize the storyboard UML class called "Genotyping"). The PharmacogenomicsTesting act can have any number of the Genotype module (supposed to be a CMET), in two modes:

  • downward PharmacogenomicsTesting we see the component association with the Genotype module, where the actual genomic result data reside;
  • to the left of PharmacogenomicsTesting we can see an association with the GeneSelection act which is a fundamental part of a Pharmacogenomics clinical trial. To represent the genes that were selected we also use the Genotype module, but populate only the gene identifiers, while in the trial-result-Genotype-modules we can populate any part of the Genotype model (e.g., sequencing, gene expression, SNPs, haplotypes, etc.).

The PharmacogenomicsTesting is performed by a DNALab which is a role played by the Collaborator entity (these objects are shown to the right of the act).

 2.1.3.1Tissue Typing (POCG_SN000010UV01)

Overview

Over the last decade there is a shift in the routine of typing testing laboratories, from serological based HLA typing to molecular based DNA techniques. With a greater understanding of the extent and relevance of HLA variability, there has been a move towards increasingly higher levels of resolution in the tissue typing laboratory. DNA Typing relates to a number of lab methods whose results allow DNA similarities of donor-recipient, familial relationships or any two persons to be established for the purpose of transplantation of bone-marrow and organs, or in forensic scenarios. It is done by comparing the characteristic polymorphic patterns in the Human Leucocytes Antigens (HLA). The main facility for using such methods is a tissue typing laboratory.

In most HLA typing applications, it is useful to have allele identification at higher levels of resolution. For example, donor-recipient typing in transplant procedures, disease association or anthropology studies should all be performed at the highest level of resolution that is practical. In cases of finding a donor for a patient who needs to go through bone-marrow transplantation, the DNA typing results provide with better matching accuracy and might support the prediction of GVH (graft-versus-host) disease after transplantation or GVM (Graft Versus Malignancy) effect in the new trend called 'mini-transplant'. In forensic scenarios it is used in paternity disputes or to check evidences from the crime scene against suspects' DNA. In forensic scenarios even lower resolution methods were accepted so far, but given the new methods accuracy it's likely to see increasing demand for DNA typing data that better eliminate any doubt.

The information generated with direct DNA sequencing of HLA genes is qualitatively different than with other DNA- based typing methods. Obtaining the complete sequence of a gene of interest at the nucleotide level provides the opportunity to assign allele typing at higher resolution. Sequencing-based typing is one of the latest technologies applied to histocompatibility testing providing the highest level of definition and resolution in class I and II HLA typing. DNA sequencing is now used in routine HLA typing through recent advances in automated DNA sequencers and simplified sequencing chemistry. These advances have allowed greater sample throughput, providing a realistic, practical, and preferred alternative to other DNA methods, while offering a far higher level of resolution.

Triggers

  • Tissue Typing for Organ and Bone-Marrow Transplantation

  • Identify people who may be at risk for certain autoimmune diseases

  • Forensic triggers: crime suspects; paternity test; anthropology studies; ethnic crimes

Presentation

Tina Transplant is referred to BMT (Bone Marrow Transplant) Center for transplant workup. Blood samples are drawn from Tina and her first degree relatives. The samples are forwarded to the Tissue Typing Lab with the order.

Tissue Typing Lab performs Class I HLA testing. Matches at Class I are further typed for Class II. A matching analysis is prepared and the results report is returned to the ordering Center.

BMT Center evaluates results:

  • Best Outcome: Match with First-Degree Relative(s)

    • Matching relative approached for consent and donation.

  • Alternate Outcome: No Match with First-Degree Relative(s), Distant Relatives Available

    • BMT Center determines whether typing of more distant relatives should be considered. If marriages between relatives occurred, or Tina has common haplotypes, distant relatives may be approached for consent and sample. BMT Center sends blood sample from distant relative to Tissue Typing Lab for Class I testing.

    • Tissue Typing Lab identifies matches at Class I. These matches are further typed for Class II. A matching analysis is prepared and the results report is returned to the BMT Center for evaluation. If an acceptable match is identified, relative is approached for consent and donation.

  • Alternate Outcome: No Match with Relative(s), Unrelated Donor Needed

    • BMT Center identifies candidate Donor Bank(s) and sends Tina's typing data to Donor Bank.

    • Donor Bank searches for Class I (low-resolution) matches and returns match(es) to BMT Center for evaluation.

    • BMT Center identifies best class I matches and requests DNA for those matches from Donor Bank.

    • Donor Bank retrieves DNA samples and forwards to Tissue Typing Lab.

    • Tissue Typing Lab performs Class II typing. Results are returned to BMT Center for evaluation.

    • If a promising match is found, BMT Center requests confirmatory test.

    • Donor Bank obtains new blood samples from potential donor and forwards to Tissue Typing Lab.

    • Tissue Typing Lab performs high-resolution Class I and Class II typing, and returns results to BMT Center.

    • BMT Center evaluations results and determines if suitable unrelated match has been identified. If an acceptable match is found, the unrelated donor is approached by the Donor Bank for consent and donation.

Once donor has been identified and consented, BMT Center initiates pre-transplant preparatory regimen for Tina, and obtains marrow from donor.

Data (for transplant patients)

  1. Complete medical and family history for the patients; emphasis on patients with special problems or treatments such as:

    • Patients who need repeating transplant

    • Patients who are candidates for bone-marrow "mini-transplant"

    • Patients with diseases associated with HLA (e.g., multiple sclerosis)

  2. Informed consent document

    • Supplementary educational materials

    • Electronic patient signature

  3. Lab order

    • Orderable in formulary

    • Test prerequisites

    • Protocol/workflow

  4. Lab result (for patient, donor, patient's mother and father if available)

    • Class I allele sequences: HLA-A, HLA-B, HLA-C

    • Class II allele sequences: HLA-DP, HLA-DQ, HLA-DR

    • Interpretation with explanation

    • The extent of donor-recipient matching

    • The appropriateness of the previous result to the intended treatment (e.g., the new "mini-transplant" might better succeed without full matching)

    • Annotation of sequences with interpretation details

2.2.1GeneticLocus  (POCG_RM000010UV01)

Diagram


Click thumbnail above to open larger graphic in a new window
Description
Parent:   Clinical Genomics Domain Information Model (POCG_DM000023UV)
The GeneticLocus model describes data relating to a genetic locus, which we propose to be the basic unit of genomic information exchange in healthcare. This model is not meant to be a biological model; rather it is aimed at the needs of healthcare with the vision of personalized medicine in mind. Also it could facilitate the needs of clinical research conducted within the healthcare enterprises. It is the result of our effort to look for the commonalities in each genomic-oriented storyboard that we have been working on (i.e., Tissue Typing, Cystic Fibrosis, BRCA and Pharmacogenomics). Thus, this model contains a subset of the overall Clinical Genomics DIM as it focuses on a single locus. It appears as a CMET in both the Clinical Genomics DSTU as well as in the HL7 Common Domains so that every HL7 group that needs to convey high-resolution genomic data could utilize it . The Genetic Locus might be derived and further constrained by its main subject (e.g., human, animal, and viral) or by type of genomic data (e.g., DNA, Expression and Proteomics). The trial use of this draft standard will guide the derivation process in a way that is most useful to its early adopters.

Genotype Scope and Modularity

The Genotype model evolved from the work on several storyboards that involve genomic data: Tissue Typing, Cystic Fibrosis, BRCA, and Pharmacogenomics. For example, in the BMT -tissue typing storyboard we have identified three modules of information: the first one is about the messages and documents being exchanged in the BMT tissue-typing use case. The second one zooms in and describes in more detail the unique observations used in tissue-typing, i.e., the individual tissue-typing observation and the matching observation which indicates the level of matching between two individual tissue-typing observations (e.g., patient and donor). The rationale for this modularity is that the tissue-typing observation could be used in other use cases such as fatherhood testing and forensic cases. The third zoom-in is about the individual genotype that describes a pair of alleles from the same chromosomal locus and is the center of this document.

Figure 1 describes the containment of the above-mentioned modules. It is expected that the genotype module will be plugged into other use cases such as genetic diagnostic and clinical trials. The same design analysis was conducted with the other storyboards with the focus on making the Genotype module a shared model that can be then used in different messages that will be created to serve the unique business logic of each of the genomic-orient storyboards.



Figure 1: Modularity in using the Genotype models

Main characteristics:

  • The entry point to this model is a GeneticLocus observation which could be associated with a pair of alleles on paternal and maternal homologous chromosomes.

  • Core observations associated directly with each allele are Sequence, Sequence Variation and Expression. These core classes are also the ones which encapsulate raw genomic data.
  • In addition, sequence, sequence variation and expression data could be associated directly with the GeneticLocus observation if data is not available at the allelic level granularity.
  • In addition to the core classes, other classes hold extracted and derived data such certain types of variations, expression main results, references to as set of loci (e.g., a haplotype that this gene belongs to), and proteomics data (e.g., determinant peptides).
  • Both the GeneticLocus and IndividualAllele observations can recurse to represent relationships to genes/alleles from other loci (e.g., in a biological pathway).
  • The Sequence class could recurse as well to allow the representation of the translational path, i.e., DNA, RNA and Protein sequences, derived from each other.

Model Walk-Through:

Notes:

* The Use of the 'id', 'code' and 'value' Attributes in the Genotype Model:

The use of these attributes in the various Genotype classes depends on the extent to which the data has being personalized and how different are the results from the known genome. It is also different in those classes that encapsulate raw genomic data. For example, in the IndividualAllele class, in the case that the patient's allele was fully sequenced and found to be slightly different than the one registered in GenBank or other reference databases, there is no external code to place in one of those attributes, rather the IndividualAllele class is associated with the Sequence class where the individual sequence could be placed in the value attribute. If it is a new allele indeed, temporary identifiers could be placed in the id attribute until it is registered externally. If, however, that is a known allele, then the 'value' attribute can be populated with the appropriate code from GenBank for example. In this case there isn't much point in populating the Sequence class as it can be retrieved from GenBank, but for self-containment purposes in a specific implementation, it could be that the GenBank sequence will be copied and placed in the Sequence class. Overall, the 'id' attribute should be used to uniquely identifying that specific instance (possibly using the LSID format). The 'code' attribute should identify the kind of data stored in the 'value' attribute, and the 'value' attribute should hold actual data, for example, a genotype characteristic (e.g., heterozygous) or an external gene code from GenBank, dbSNP. In the 'encapsulating' classes (e.g., Sequence, Expression, etc.) the 'value' attribute should hold the bioinformatics markup itself. In the latter case, the code should hold an indication of the exact bioinformatics format used to populate the 'value' attribute.

    • Expression

      The class Expression is a generalization of all types of expression data (RNA and protein). Its code attribute identifies the type of expression data it carries. This class is one of the Genotype's encapsulating classes, that is, it holds in its value attribute portions of relevant bioinformatics markup (e.g., MAGE-ML for gene expression data), complying with constrained schemas of the full-fledged markups.   • Other Classes and Proteomics

    • GeneticLoci

      The Haplotype class was replaced by the GeneticLoci class which is a generalization of a haplotype and other sets of loci. The class could carry essential data about the loci or just hold an id of Genetic Loci instance complying with the HL7 Clinical Genomics Genetic Loci model (see further on in this document).

      GeneticLoci Attributes:




      • Translocation

        This class was removed and the term was added to the vocabulary of the AssociatedProperty class associated with the SequenceVariation class.

    • Polypeptide and DeterminantPeptide

      The Sequence class could be associated with its resulted or corresponding polypeptides (represented by the Polypeptide class) as well as with the determinant peptides (represented by the DeterminantPeptide class). Note that the Sequence class has a recursive relation and it is possible to nest an RNA sequence within a DNA sequence, and a protein sequence within an RNA sequence. The Polypeptide could then be associated with the protein sequences or directly with any of the above levels. Also, it is possible to associate the DeterminantPeptide with the Polypeptide class or directly with the Sequence class. Both classes (Polypeptide and DeterminantPeptide) could be associated with several instances of the ClinicalPhenotype classes.

      The proteomics classes in this model represent protein data that one is deriving from the sequences (by means of computational biology) and is not measuring directly in the subject. The latter could be represented as regular lab results (using the HL7 Lab specs), which could be referenced in the GeneticLocus instance as if they were phenotype observations.

      A common case for the use of proteomics in this model could be as follows: Checking whether an amino acid change would result from the variant; if so - whether the new amino acid change is to an amino acid of a different size or charge state that would likely change the shape of the active region of the protein; how far the change is from the active site; whether the change is in a regulator region, and so forth. These observations could then be associated to phenotypic data.

      For example, consider the following case described in OMIM: "Despite the dramatic responses to EGFR inhibitors in patients with non-small cell lung cancer, most patients ultimately have a relapse. Kobayashi et al. (2005) reported a patient with EGFR-mutant, gefitinib-responsive, advanced non-small cell lung cancer who had a relapse after 2 years of complete remission during treatment with gefitinib. The DNA sequence of the EGFR gene in his tumor biopsy specimen at relapse revealed the presence of a second mutation {131550.0006}. Structural modeling and biochemical studies showed that this second mutation led to the gefitinib resistance." (OMIM *131550)

  • To Phenotype and Beyond...

    • ClinicalPhenotype

      The 'wrapper' class ClinicalPhenotype is associated with the SequenceVariation class and includes a choice of populating an ObservedClinicalPhenotype class internally (i.e., within the Genotype instance), externally (ExternalObservedClinicalPhenotype) or pointing to a KnownClinicalPhenotype. Note that this class is just a 'warpper' (i.e., classCode = "ActContainer") of the actual phenotypes and thus has no attributes of its own.

    • ExternalObservedClinicalPhenotype

      The difference between ObservedClinicalPhenotype and ExternalObservedClinicalPhenotype is that the former represents a phenotype that the genomic data creator chose to include within the genotype instance itself, while the latter represents a phenotype that exists externally to the genotype instance, for example residing in the patient record's problem list. This class has an id (mandatory attribute) that serves as a pointer to the target observation. Note that the typeCode attribute is set to ActRelationshipExternalReference domain which is an "x_" value set dedicated to representing types of external references. For example, it could be set to "XCRPT" which means that the source observation is an excerpt from the target observation. In our case, this could be used when you might want to refer to a clinical observation in a patient's medical record, but also would like to show a few of its essential data items in the GeneticLocus instance (in the ClinicalPhenotype object).

    • KnownClinicalPhenotype

      The difference between KnownClinicalPhenotype and ObservedClinicalPhenotype is that while observed phenotype means a phenotype that was observed in the patient, KnownClinicalPhenotype is in fact a piece od knowledge, i.e., it is known to be associated with the genomic data but not necessarily in this specifc patient to whom the genomic data belongs. The class KnownClinicalPhenotype has a definitional mood (i.e., taken from or pointing to a description of such a disease in some form of a master file, ontology, lookup table, etc., separated from the patient data) to reflect the difference from the event mood of the ObservedClinicalPhenotype class.

    • pertinentInformation

      This ActRelationship class (named pertinentInformation) represents the association of a genomic observation with a number of phenotype (clinical) observations. Its mandatory attribute typeCode holds the semantics of what is the type of this association. It is defined as <=PERT which means that any code in the PERT sub-hierarchy of the HL7 ActRelationshipType Vocabulary is permitted here. For a full list of these codes, please refer to the Vocabulary chapter in the HL7 V3 Ballot Package. Appendix E lists codes from all branches of the ActRelationshipType Vocabulary (PERT and others) that seem possible candidates for use in Clinical Genomics. This work is still under progress and we might propose this list as well as our own codes to be a new x_domain in ActRelationshipType Vocabulary.

Miscellaneous Issues

  • Association types:

    Association types (ActRelationship typeCode) are consistent with the following principles:

    • Encapsulating classes are components (COMP typeCode) of the IndividualAllele and GeneticLocus classes, while...
    • Bubbled-up classes are derivations (DRIV typeCode) of encapsulating classes;
    • Clinical phenotypes are pertinent to (PERT typeCode) genomic classes (Note: in previous versions this was fixed to "caused by" but it was too restricted. The code CAUS is part of the PERT sub-vocabulary and thus could be still used, but other codes are available as well)
    • Non patient-specific data items are defined (INST typeCode) by classes with mood code = DEF (definitional), that is, defined and described outside of the patient medical records, in some kind of master file, dictionary , ontology, etc.;
    • Alleles in one GeneticLocus refer (REFR typeCode) to alleles in another locus. Nevertheless, we develop a vocabulary for such types of relationship, which will be proposed for RIM harmonization as a new domain in ActRelationshipType Vocabulary (see table 16 for a first draft).

  • Observation Interpretation vs. Observation Relationships:

    There is a subtle distinction between observation interpretation codes and ActRelationship types between genomic and clinical observations. The observation interpretation is in fact a piece of knowledge, not necessarily shown as phenotypes of that genotype in the patient. If the interpretation code is DELETERIOUS (see table 8) then the observation could be complemented by associating it to the KnownAssociatedDisease class which is also a non-patient specific data in DEF mood Code. In contrast, the phenotype is an actual observation valid for this patient, which is associated with a genomic observation.

  • Bioinformatics formats:

    In general, we use bioinformatics formats in the Genotype model to feature the encapsulation of raw genomic data such as sequencing, expression and proteomic data. To enable the embedding of such data accepted from labs that work with bioinformatics formats, it is possible to assign specific XML portions into the Sequence and Expression value attributes (as well as into SequenceVariation). This encapsulation of 'foreign' markup is made possible due to the use of the HL7 ED (Encapsulating Data) Data Type which is defined as follows: "ED holds data that is primarily intended for human interpretation or for further machine processing which is outside the scope of HL7. ED includes unformatted or formatted written language, multimedia data, or structured information as defined by a different standard (e.g., XML-signatures.)"

    The use of the XML bioinformatics markups is restricted, that is, not all tags are allowed, rather only a subset which relates to a specific patient and include the information pertinent to healthcare. The restrictions on those external XML standards are specified elsewhere but a draft of a constrained BSML schema for sequencing data is presented in Appendix C. For more details about the rationale behind this mixture of HL7 and bioinformatics markup, see the section "Coexistence of HL7 Classes and Bioinformatics Markup".

  • Validation:

    The use of external markup in HL7 messages requires that a receiver of an HL7 instance that contains a Genotype instance, will carry out a 'double-validation' process: first step is to validate the instance against the HL7 message specification (of which the Genotype schema is part of) and the second phase is to validate the content of those value attributes against their respective content models. The valid content models of the Sequence and Expression value attributes will be an integral part of the entire Genotype specification, but at this point it is still considered informative.

  • Associated Properties / Observations and the Harmonization Proposals:

    In previous versions we have coped with the reluctance of both the HL7 RIM Harmonization process and the HL7 Clinical Genomics group to nail down common attributes of genomic observations by adding new classes and attributes to the RIM, by elaborating on the SequenceVariation and Expression properties and creating two new Observation classes (SequenceVariationProperty and ExpressionProperty) to be placeholders for each of theses properties. For example, the proposed 'length' property of a possible SequenceVariation new RIM class could be represented by an object of the SequenceVariationProperty class which only has code and value attributes. The code will indicate that this observation describes the position of the variation and the value attribute holds the position itself. The assumption is that this observation is an integral part of the parent observation with the same effective time. It could be identified only by going through the source variation object. In contrast, we also had the LocusAssociatedObservation class which is a place holder for associated observations such as copy number, zygosity, dominancy and gene family. These observations are independent observations that do have an id, effective time and method code.

    In this update #1 of the approved DSTU, the associated properties/observation classes were consolidated to two classes: AssociatedProperty and AssociatedObservation. Instead of having specific class names (e.g., SequenceVariationProperty), all core classes now have these two generic classes coming off them. It makes the model simpler (but put the burden on the parsing to understand the context of each associated property/observation). The basic difference between associated properties and associated observations is that an associated property should have been (and eventually may be) part of the parent class attributes. It's an inherent part of the parent observation and thus doesn't have id, time stamp, method, performer, etc. They 'inherent' all these attributes from their parent. An associated observation, in contrast, is an independent observation and a component of its parent class.

Coexistence of HL7 Objects and Bioinformatics Markup:

When exploring the Genotype model one could identify the use of bioinformatics markup such as MAGE for gene expression and BSML for DNA sequencing. Also, a few of the HL7 Classes such as the property classes are overlapping the elements of the bioinformatics markup in a way that it is possible to find a SNP represented in both the Sequence class as well as in the SequenceVariationProperty class. The question then arises, what are the relationships of the two and how do they coexist? The following are a few points to note about that issue:

  • HL7 mission is to develop message/document specs that will be used in healthcare practice. The mission of the HL7 Clinical Genomics SIG is to develop message/document specs where genomic data are involved (e.g., Tissue Typing, Genetic Testing, and Clinical Trials).
  • Bioinformatics communities develop models/markups and are usually not ANSI-Accredited SDOs and thus cannot sanction and maintain these formats. Naturally, their orientation is more towards research and the needs of information exchange between research facilities, data mining and statistical analysis tools.
  • The HL7 Clinical Genomics SIG attempts to constrain existing bioinformatics markups and embed them in the HL7 model. This effort brings up the issue of overlapping between the HL7 core Reference Information Model (RIM) classes & data types from which we derive the Genotype model and embedded bioinformatics markups. One approach is not allowing overlaps. For example, if we model a SNP in an HL7 class, and also allow BSML extractions to be embedded in the Sequence class, then we should constrain the BSML extractions so that it will not hold SNP data (in the Isoform tag), rather those parts of the BSML (if exist) should be transformed and moved to HL7 classes.
  • Another approach is seeing the overlap as beneficial: The bioinformatics markups are kind of raw data that might not always get into the HL7 Clinical Genomics actual instances, rather might be only referenced from the HL7 instance as supporting evidences. When it does get into an HL7 instance then we have a blend of HL7 classes and embedded genomic markup. This situation is analogous to the raw imaging data. The average referring physician doesn't dig into the dozens of CT images taken in a study, rather look at the already designated ROIs or just read the radiologist report.
  • A similar situation might exist in the Clinical Genomics specification: many clinicians might not be able to grasp the raw genomic data represented in the bioinformatics markup but it could be helpful to have parts of it in the HL7 instances for purposes like evidence availably and machine-processabilty. The HL7 classes should be seen as representing the digest of the raw genomic data which is most pertinent to the healthcare practice itself. There is a space here for applications that might parse the bioinformatics markup, intelligently populate the HL7 classes and then associate them with the appropriate clinical data.
  • The HL7 classes in the Genotype model have the advantage of being better tied with the other HL7 classes in the patient record (e.g., in a problem/allergy list) and thus serves better the ability to link individual genomic data to the clinical data of that individual. Note that bioinformatics models also include clinical data so this poses an overlapping problem in the other direction.
  • The integration applications that recognize bioinformatics markups could integrate various genomic sources into healthcare standards used for patient care, so there will not be one winning format necessarily. The issue should not be which bioinformatics model is the "best fit with HL7 Reference Information Model", rather, how could we develop mechanisms to digest data in various representations and link them to the HL7 RIM for the benefit of personalized medicine.
  • For example, the BSML markup is fairly simple and the HL7 SIG has done some work on BSML in constraining it to be embedded in HL7 classes. XML samples are available to show how the BSML is embedded in an HL7 message of the tissue typing use case. The constraining process of BSML made sure that data items related to presentation elements are not included and that the instance includes one and only one patient's data and this patient is uniquely identified. In contrast, PharmaGKB has a repository orientation like GenBank with actual data available for retrieval. This could be a data source of generic knowledge that can be accessed by healthcare information systems. In HL7 these repositories are usually treated as controlled coding schemes. They need to be recognized by the HL7 Vocabulary Committee and then any HL7 instance can reference terms, identifiers and data from that taxonomy.

For illustration of the latter scenario, see sample on the EGRF (described in the samples appendix B), courtesy of the NHANES project carried out in the USA CDC.

Parent:   Clinical Genomics Domain Information Model (POCG_DM000023UV)
The GeneticLoci model allows the representation of data relating to a set of loci along the DNA material. The set of loci could be of differnet types, for example, a haplotype (allele or SNP), a genetic profile, a biological pathway, a set of genetic test results which contains results of multiple genes, etc. The Genetic Loci model utlizes the Genetic Locus model as a CMET.

Model Walk-Through:

  • Entry Point and Locus/Gene/Allele Classes:

    • GeneticLoci

      The entry point is a GeneticLoci class allowing the representation of the type of this loci group (e.g., allele haplotype like in tissue typing of HLA antigens) and an optional code for identifying the loci set (if available).

      GeneticLoci Attributes:




      • LociChoice

        A GeneticLoci instance consists of zero to many GeneticLocus CMETs or other GeneticLoci classes. This recursive structure allows the representation of a complex set of genetic loci as comprised of 'sub' genetic loci sets. The actual genetic/genomic data is represented through GeneticLocus instances at any level of nesting required.

      • AssociatedObservation

        The AssociatedObservation class associated with GeneticLoci is a place holder for various observations related to the set of loci that have been observed independently of the parent observation. This is a generic catcher for data that can be placed in any other class in this model. Population of the code & value attributes of this class is controlled by a vocabulary which is still under development.

      • Interpretation

        In cases where there is an interpretation to the entire set of loci data, it is possible to use the interpretationCode attribute of the GeneticLoci class or populate the associated phenotypes (the phenotype model is identical to that in the GeneticLocus model and might be packaged as an internal CMET for better modularity).

      • Raw genomic data and the value attribute:

        While the value attribute typically holds a common code of this set of loci, it can also hold raw genomic data for the entire set of loci. For example, if the set of loci is a gene expression assay, the value attribute can hold the part of the MAGE-ML xml that holds data on all loci.

  • Other Classes

    • GeneticReportDocument

      A report document that summarizes the data of a genetic loci set could be associated with the GeneticLoci class and is of type DOCCLIN which represents in HL7 a CDA type (Clinical Document Architecture). It is possible to actually embed an entire CDA document in the text attribute of the GeneticReportDocument class or just point to this document through the id attribute. Other related reports (e.g., follow-up's, addenda) could be associated as well. For more details about the use of clinical documents, please refer to the CDA and the Medical Records specification in the HL7 V3 Ballot Package.

      • sequelTo

        This is a recursive association to the GeneticReportDocument class, which allows the representation of several documents that relate to one another and to the Genetic loci of course. For example, an addendum or follow-up document to the first summary.

    • Participants

      In general, a group of optional participants are associated with both the GeneticLoci and the GeneticTestOrder classes allowing the recording of participants of the order act as well as the results fulfilling that order:

      • recordTarget

        The record target indicates whose medical record holds the documentation of this act (i.e., the order or the results). This is especially important when the subject of a service is not the patient himself. Note that the subject can be overridden in certain GeneticLocus instances. This could be useful when describing for example genomic data relating to various specimens (healthy and tumor tissues) or relating to virus, as part of a genetic testing of a patient who carries this virus.

      • author

        Author of the data.

      • performer

        Performer of the observations.

      • verifier

        Verifier of the observations.

      • informationRecipient

        To whom the data should be sent.

The GeneticTestOrder model (for comments only)

General:

The GeneticLoci class might be representing a fulfillment of a test order. For that purpose we work with the HL7 Lab SIG to see how our models could be embedded in their messages. Nevertheless, the new GeneticTestOrder model (presented here for comments only at this point) is an attempt to 'roll-out' the Lab order model and take those classes that we need. In addition it serves as a 'gap analysis' to see if the Lab model can accommodate all the genetic testing requirements. In cases where gaps encountered, there are additions to this model whose origins cannot found in the Lab model.

Therefore, this model serves our dialog with the Lab SIG, as well as a starting point for a generic implementation guide on how to use the Lab model for genetic testing purposes. It could be that this model will be balloted in future ballot cycles, depending on feedback we get from ballotters, early adopters and other stakeholders.

GeneticTestOrder class:

The entry point is a GeneticTestOrder class allowing the representation of a genetic test order. It's an observation in RQO (Request) mood code. It is (or may be) fulfilled by zero to many GeneticLoci CMETs (see below for further description on how to represent the result of this order). As aforementioned, this class and its associations are also a "roll-out" of the ObservationRequest class in the A_PlacerOrder CMET of the HL7 Lab Result model.

Attributes of the GeneticTestOrder class

  • id: Instance identifier used to uniquely identify the GeneticTestOrder instance.
  • Code: The GeneticTestOrder.code attribute can hold a code denoting the type of test, for example - an HLA haplotype or a genes panel for a specific disease, etc.
  • text: Free-text description, including a reference mechanism.
  • effectiveTime: Time at which the genetic test was ordered.
  • confidentialityCode: A code that controls the disclosure of information about this genetic test order. Vocabulary- The HL7 Vocabulary "Confidentiality" with possible extensions from the Clinical Genomics group (TBD).
  • reasonCode: A code specifying the motivation, cause, or rationale of the ordering this genetic test.
  • statusCode The status of this genetic test order. Based on the HL7 Vocabulary "ActStatus" with possible extensions from the Clinical Genomics group (TBD).
  • methodCode: The method(s) by which this genetic test should be performed (note - this is optional and even if exists - doesn't necessarily imply the actual method used (which is conveyed through the results in the GeneticLoci and GeneticLocus instances). Vocabulary- the HL7 Vocabulary "ObservationMethod" with possible extensions from the Clinical Genomics group (TBD).

Associations

  • GeneticLoci CMET:

    The GeneticLoci CMET itself can be in request or event mood. If it's in request mood, it serves the representation of requested observation, e.g., the test order is about requesting to find specific sequence variations in certain genes. If it's in event mood, then it represents the actual findings/results of the genetic testing.

  • In general, there are four types of data that can be associated with the test order and they are represented by the following association classes coming off the GeneticTestOrder class:

    • component

      This is a recursive association back to GeneticTestOrder to allow the representation of '-sub-orders' as components of a complex order.

    • sequelTo

      This is a recursive association back to GeneticTestOrder, allowing representation of reflex genetic tests.

    • definition

      This association connects the order to a master service catalog for elaboration on further details of the ordered test.

    • pertinentInformation

      This association connects to the A_SupportingClinicalInformation CMET, which allows the representation of patient specific data that is important to accompany the test order. This CMET might be replaced by the Clinical Statement model, under development in HL7.
